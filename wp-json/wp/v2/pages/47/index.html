\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 Simulation has become a valued staple of mass-media entertainment, but originated far before then in the medias of photography and painting, and continues today as a desire for verisimilitude. While this desire has been repeatedly contested in the history of paintings, it is currently unquestioned in gaming, CGI and VR. Yet within fine art circles, the desire for new technology and interactivity has insinuated its way even into contemporary painting. Although I am interested in the rapid growth of technology, I\u2019m also interested in the survival of painting tropes in newer forms. By confusing the expectations for art, entertainment, and technology, my work questions how the boundaries between them are constructed and upheld in a world where all three are quickly evolving.<\/p>

\u00a0<\/p>

\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0\u00a0 The process of my paintings are worked in tandem with my digital work. First I\u00a0 create 3D models of real-world objects through the use of photo-grammetry and 3D modelling programs such as Blender and Meshroom, which are then integrated into virtual scenes created in the game engine Unity. All of the programs I utilize are free to use and have only become accessible within the last several years, which has led to their exponential growth within art communities. Next, I transfer these scenes via projector and paint onto the canvas, which is stretched at the same curvature used for gaming monitors and historic theater stages. It is my hope that these works will inspire the viewer to consider the relationship between our quickly evolving technology and our common desires that have repeated through history.<\/p>

The group exhibition Synesthesia<\/em> is an exploration of Arduino and interactive art. This interactive exhibit invites the viewer to stand before a transparent screen displaying several photo-grammatized floral arrangements. The arrangements react to the position of the viewer in the room, turning to face them, and bringing a real-world plant through a digital wringer and back to the gallery.<\/p>

Outside.In and its acompanying exhibition Inside.Out hosted in person by the Western Washington Viking Union Gallery, are a part of the continual discussion of redefining the gallery experience in light of the Covid-19 pandemic. Featuring interactive photo-grammetized copies of real-world sculptures by artists Henry Watts, Amanda Jenkins, and others, the website makes sculpture works accessible outside the usual gallery walls. This exhibition aims to consider all aspects of an online gallery as a part of the art, rather than just as means to solve a temporary problem as many ‘digital exhibitions’ have so reacted.<\/p>
